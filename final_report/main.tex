\section{Introduction}

For this project, the team has created software that identifies and classifies idiomatic expressions and literary devices in English text. Specifically, this task
focuses on detecting and labelling the type of figurative language present in short text segments, including: metaphors, similes, personification, as well as a few others. \\

Understanding idiomatic and figurative language is a fundamental challenge in natural language processing because the intended meaning often cannot be
derived directly from the literal meanings of the individual words. While humans can usually recognize these expressions using context and world knowledge,
computational models struggle due to ambiguity, cultural conventions, and the subtle differences between similar devices (for example, discerning a metaphor vs. an idiom).\\

This task is significant because idiomatic language appears frequently in real world applications of natural language processing such as social media analysis, educational tools, and conversational agents. Misinterpreting these expressions can lead to incorrect or misleading system responses. Unlike standard sentiment analysis, which focuses primarily on emotional polarity, this task requires the software to perform semantic disambiguation and contextual reasoning. This goes beyond surface-level text classification, and requires the model to navigate linguistic ambiguity where the intended meaning cannot be derived directly from the literal meanings of individual words.\\

\section{Related Work}

The team found their dataset attached to a study done in 2022, which performed the same task. This study was incredibly helpful as a benchmark, as it tested for the same categories as this task. This was the first indication that BERT would perform quite well, as in this study it outperformed both Multinomial Naive-Bayes and Support Vector Machine models \citet{adewumi2022potentialidiomaticexpressionpieenglish}. The BERT resultant F1-Score was 0.948, which this team would later surpass. \\

A study performed in 2009 examined the results of a combined classifier that uses both supervised and unsupervised models to examine literal vs. non-literal use of idiomatic expressions. This study found that the combined classifier was more accurate than the unsupervised classifier alone and the majority baseline; the combined classifier resulted in an error reduction of over 35\% \cite{li-sporleder-2009-classifier}. \\

Research by \citet{11251805} highlighted the success of the BERT model when applied to a text to analyse literary devices. Models such as GloVe and Word2Vec treat words as separate entities, while BERT takes in the surrounding context, leading to much better performance analysing literary devices. This paper reported an F1-Score of 0.85 resulting from applying BERT to detect metaphors on a corpus build from Project Gutenberg and other public literary anthologies. \\

A similar paper published in 2024 tested the BERT model on text classification, sentiment analysis, and and question answering. This paper found that BERT yielded an F1-Score on text classification of 0.915, a score on sentiment analysis of 0.885, and a score on question answering of 0.865. These results were all higher than their counterparts performed by recurrent neural networks (RNNs), supporting the conclusion that the BERT model would outperform RNN methods, at the cost of much longer training time and computational load \cite{10918499}. \\

A study was done by \citet{wu2024researchapplicationdeeplearningbased} that compared BERT and DistilBERT while performing sentiment analysis. It explains that by updating parameter weights used on the BERT model, DistilBERT can perform better on sentence classification when using a full set of parameters. \\

\section{Dataset}

\subsection{Source and Collection}
The dataset utilized for this project is the Potential Idiomatic Expression (PIE)-English Corpus, which is a labeled dataset designed for the classification of idiomatic expressions and literary devices. The data is publicly available via a GitHub repository and is derived primarily from the British National Corpus (BNC), which accounts for 96.9\% of the samples, and UK Web Pages (UKWaC), which accounts for the remaining 3.1\%. The original corpus was extracted by near-native English speakers and labeled by independent annotators to ensure accuracy. 

\subsection{Dataset Properties and Labels}
The task is defined as a single-label classification problem where each text segment is mapped to one of ten primary literary device or idiomatic categories. The ten classes include: Euphemism, Hyperbole, Irony, Literal, Metaphor, Oxymoron, Paradox, Parallelism, Personification, and Simile. 

A critical characteristic of this dataset is the significant class imbalance. As shown in Table \ref{tab:label_dist}, the distribution is heavily skewed toward Metaphors, while some categories, such as Irony and Oxymoron, represent less than 1\% of the total data points. This distribution presents a notable challenge for model training, as the minority labels have very low frequency.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
The original source data was provided in a token-per-row CSV format. To make the data suitable for sentence-level classification and manual annotation, we implemented a Python script to perform the following preprocessing steps:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sentence Reconstruction:} Tokens were joined to reconstruct full sentences from the tokenized rows.
    \item \textbf{Normalization:} All reconstructed sentences were converted to lowercase.
    \item \textbf{Random Sampling:} From the total 20,174 samples, we randomly sampled 1,200 data points to create a balanced mixture for our team's manual annotation phase.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Annotation Procedure and Reliability}
Our team followed a structured annotation protocol using a shared spreadsheet interface. Each of the four team members independently annotated 300 unique data points, resulting in 1,200 total primary annotations. To measure Inter-Annotator Agreement (IAA), each member also re-annotated a subset of 45 data points originally labeled by a teammate.

The consistency of our annotations was evaluated using Cohen's Kappa for pairwise comparisons and Krippendorff's Alpha for overall group reliability. Our pairwise Cohen's Kappa scores ranged from 0.6293 to 0.8990, representing substantial to great agreement. The final Krippendorff's Alpha was 0.8790, which actually exceeded some individual pairwise scores, suggesting our group was highly aligned with the original corpus' labels.

\subsection{Data Observations}
During the annotation process, we identified several interesting linguistic nuances. Some sentences presented "edge cases" where literal and metaphorical meanings overlapped, such as \textit{"the worry at the back of her mind,"} which is metaphorical, appearing alongside the literal phrase \textit{"she sat down beside her patient"}. Furthermore, because the corpus is British-centric, we encountered regional idioms such as \textit{"not by a long chalk,"} which is the UK equivalent of the North American \textit{"not by a long shot"}. These cultural variations and internal sentence ambiguities highlight the difficulty of the classification task for automated models.


\section{Features}

\subsection{Model Inputs and Representation}
The primary input for our models consists of reconstructed English sentences derived from the token-per-row CSV format of the PIE-English Corpus. During the preprocessing phase, these tokens are joined to form complete grammatical units, ensuring the models have access to the full sentential context necessary for disambiguating figurative language. To reduce the complexity of the input space and handle vocabulary sparsity, all text is normalized to lowercase before being fed into the embedding layers.

\subsection{Representation Learning and Embeddings}
Our team utilizes a combination of learned and
 pretrained embeddings. For our transformer-based models, 
 we perform representation learning where the model learns 
 task-specific embeddings through fine-tuning.This approach
  is vital because the meaning of an idiom often cannot 
  be derived from individual word embeddings alone. 
  By fine-tuning, the model develops a representation 
  that prioritizes the semantic relationship between words
   in a figurative context.


\subsection{Feature Engineering}

For our final implementation, we shifted away from manual 
feature engineering to focus on automated representation 
learning. Rather than defining specific linguistic 
markers, we utilize contextualized word embeddings from a
 fine-tuned transformer model (DistilBERT) to 
 capture the complex semantic relationships
  inherent in idiomatic language. This approach allows
   the neural network to internally learn the necessary
    features to distinguish between literal and figurative
     meanings without the need for manually defined rules. This
      is particularly critical for our task because
       the intended meaning of an idiom often cannot 
       be derived from the literal definitions of
        individual tokens.

\subsection{Feature Selection and Data Augmentation}
The PIE-English Corpus presents a significant challenge
 due to its extreme class imbalance, with metaphors 
 representing approximately 72.7\% of the total samples. After
  evaluating our approach, we decided against performing 
  specific feature selection or data augmentation. 
  Instead, we utilized the entire dataset of 20,174 samples
   for training and evaluation to ensure the models were
    exposed to the maximum possible number of instances for 
  minority labels, such as paradoxes and ironies. 
  This decision ensures that our models learn from 
  the natural distribution of the data while
   maintaining exposure to the rarest but arguabally more relevant
   categories in the corpus.
\section{Implementation}

The simple baseline for our model was a majority class classifier. In the implementation, a class was initialized with the \texttt{most\_frequent} strategy on sklearn's \texttt{DummyClassifier}. The data is loaded into training dataframes and testing dataframes. The training data is split into the sentences and labels, which is then loaded into the classifier. The test dataframe is then loaded into the predict function and then exported to a CSV. The most frequent label in our dataset is Metaphor. \\

Our simple trained model baseline used TF-IDF to vectorize the tokens, then trained a logistic regression model. The data was built very similarly to the simple baseline in terms of importing data, fitting the model, predicting, and exporting. However, to simplify the code, a pipeline was built that took in the data, vectorized it with TF-IDF, and then trained the logistic regression model as well at once. \\
	
  The next model that was fine-tuned was the ModernBert Base model. Training, validation, and test CSVs were loaded into Pandas data frames, the labels were then encoded, and the data frames were passed into HuggingFace datasets. The model, tokenizer, and compute metrics were then initialized, parameters were passed through the training arguments, the trainer is initialized, and once the training was done, the model predicts and exports a CSV. Training was done on Google Colab using Nvidia T4 GPUs, after many hyperparameter tunings the learning rate was set to 5e-5, train batch to 16, evaluation batch to 32, 6 epochs, warm up ration of 01, weight decay to 0.01, and the best model was set to be loaded at the end. The model was then retrained with 8 epochs and included the validation data as training data and this produced our best results. \\
	
  The following models were built similarly, just without the retraining with the validation data at the end. All the models we used were BERT models because they get a better understanding of the context of sentences. \\
	
  The second model we trained was a Bert Base Uncased model. The training arguments were learning rate as 2e-5, train batch size is 16, evaluation batch size is 16, 3 epochs, 0.01 weight decay, and the best model was loaded at the end. \\
	
  The third model was a RoBERTa model with a learning rate of 1.5e-5, train batch size is 16, evaluation batch size is 32, 6 epochs, 0.01 weight decay, 0.1 warmup ratio, and the best model was loaded at the end. Fp16 was enabled to speed up training time and reduce memory usage. \\
	
  The fourth model was DeBERTa, though it produced poor results at the beginning, after fine-tuning the parameters, the results improved exponentially. The learning rate was set to 1.5e-5, train batch was 16, evaluation batch was 32, 6 epochs, warm up ratio set to 0.1, weight decay was 0.01, the best model was loaded at the end, and bf16 and fp16 were enabled for faster and more efficient training. \\

\section{Results}

The simple baseline model received an accuracy score of 0.73, but a Macro F1
 score of 0.08. Since the simple baseline model is majority class classifier, 
 it accurately predicts 73\% of the test data, however, because it only accurately 
 predicts the Metaphor class, the precision, recall, and F1 of all other classes is 0, 
 so when the Macro F1 is taken by averaging them all, it becomes very small as the only 
 one with weight is the one out of ten classes. \\

The logistic regression model had an accuracy of 0.87 and a Macro F1 score of 0.9. 
This one was much higher because the F1 score of classes other than metaphors were way 
higher than in the simple baseline model (where they were 0). By treating the text as a 
sparse "bag of words" using TF-IDF, the model successfully exploited specific vocabulary
 artifacts and trigger words unique to the minority classes. This simpler linear 
 approach effectively isolated lexical cues without being overwhelmed by broader
  sentence context. \\

The BERT Base Uncased model got an accuracy of 0.95 and a Macro F1 score of 0.63. 
Bert has 110-million parameters and was pre-trained on BooksCorpus and English 
Wikipedia over four days on 16 TPUs. It's the oldest model we used in our models. It has 0.95 
accuracy, but the 0.63 Macro F1 shows it was still affected
 by the majority class bias. Its self-attention didn't have enough depth 
 to handle the minority classes, so it often just picked "Metaphor" for anything
  complicated. \\

The RoBERTa model did better, with an accuracy of 0.97 and a Macro F1 score of 0.88. 
The model has 125-million parameter and was pre-trained on a 160GB corpus (CC-News and OpenWebText)
, using 1024 V100 GPUs. It doesn't use next-sentence 
prediction like BERT Base and used dynamic masking instead, which gives it a better understanding of semantics.
 That helped it understand minority classes like Irony and Paradox way better than base 
 BERT, boosting its Macro F1. \\

The DeBERTa model has an accuracy of 0.96 and a Macro F1 score of 0.94.
 It has 140-million parameters and was pre-trained on 78GB of data with 64 V100 GPUs.
  It uses a disentangled attention mechanism that handles word 
  content and position separately. This helps it parse complex 
  figurative language, making it better at spotting structural stuff like Similes 
  and Parallelism. \\

Finally, ModernBERT was our best classifier, it has an accuracy of 0.98 
and a Macro F1 of 0.97. It has 149-million parameter model and was pre-trained on 
2 trillion tokens from web data, code, and scientific literature. It has new features
 like Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) and unpadded Flash Attention for 
better understanding of the sentences. Combined with retraining on our full dataset (training and validation data), 
it handled the class imbalance the best, picking up on the subtle meanings of
 even the rarest figurative classes without needing obvious trigger words. \\

\section{Evaluation}

\subsection{Evaluation Strategy}
Our evaluation strategy is designed 
to assess the model's ability to
 generalize across a highly imbalanced multi-class dataset. 
 We utilize a stratified sampling approach for our 
 data splits to ensure that the minority classes, 
 such as Irony and Oxymoron, are represented in the 
 validation and test sets in proportions consistent 
 with the overall corpus. The primary goal is to 
 outperform a simple majority-vote baseline,
  which would achieve high accuracy by simply predicting
   "Metaphor" for every instance.

\subsection{Train, Validation, and Test Splits}
The dataset, consisting of 20,174 samples, was
 divided into training, validation, and test sets using
  a 60/20/20 split. The sizes of these splits are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Training Set:} 12,104 samples (60\%) used for model parameter optimization and feature learning.
\item \textbf{Validation Set:} 4,035 samples (20\%) used for hyperparameter tuning and preventing overfitting.
\item \textbf{Test Set:} 4,035 samples (20\%) held out for the final evaluation of model performance.
\end{itemize}
The label distribution remains consistent 
across these splits, with Metaphors accounting 
for roughly 72.7\% and 
the minority classes (Irony, Oxymoron, Hyperbole)
 collectively making up less than 1\%.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Due to the extreme class imbalance identified during our initial analysis, accuracy alone is an insufficient metric for this task. Instead, we prioritize the following metrics:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Macro F1-Score:} This is our primary metric, as it treats all ten classes equally, regardless of their frequency. This ensures that the model is penalized heavily if it fails to correctly identify the minority literary devices like Paradox or Irony. This is what we used to evaluate and tune. We also reported this.
\item \textbf{Accuracy:} While not the main focus, we evaluate and fine tune with. This helps with accuracy to provide a baseline comparison against the majority class classifier. However, we interpret this metric with caution due to the dataset's skewed distribution.
\item \textbf{Precision and Recall per Class:} We generate a classification report to examine specific errors, such as the model’s ability to distinguish between metaphors and similes or metaphors and literal statements.
\item \textbf{Confusion Matrix:} This qualitative tool is used to visualize which classes are most frequently confused, providing insight into the semantic overlap between similar figurative devices.
\end{itemize}

\section{Error Analysis}

To systematically examine the errors made by our models, we analyzed the confusion matrices generated for each transformer model (ModernBERT, DeBERTa, RoBERTa, and BERT Base Uncased). These matrices, detailed in the appendix, provide a quantitative view of misclassifications across the ten figurative language classes. \\

Common error patterns observed include frequent confusion between Metaphor and Literal expressions, as well as between Metaphor and Euphemism. For instance, in the ModernBERT model, out of 2901 correct Metaphor predictions, 19 were misclassified as Euphemism and 10 as Literal. This suggests semantic overlap where the boundary between figurative and literal meanings is ambiguous. Minority classes like Irony and Hyperbole showed higher error rates; for example, the BERT Base Uncased model correctly predicted 0 instances of Hyperbole, often defaulting to Metaphor. \\

Qualitatively, models struggled with sentences exhibiting subtle contextual cues. For example, the phrase "the worry at the back of her mind" was sometimes misclassified as Literal instead of Metaphor due to its metaphorical nature being context-dependent. Similarly, British idioms like "not by a long chalk" posed challenges for models not exposed to regional variations. \\

ModernBERT excelled across all classes, with high accuracy even on minority labels (e.g., 6 correct Irony predictions), demonstrating its robustness to class imbalance. In contrast, older models like BERT Base Uncased performed poorly on rare classes, highlighting the benefits of advanced architectures and larger pre-training corpora. \\

To address these issues, future work could incorporate data augmentation techniques, such as generating synthetic examples for minority classes, or loss functions like focal loss to emphasize hard-to-classify instances. We recommend prioritizing modern transformer models like ModernBERT for similar tasks, while ensuring diverse training data to capture cultural and linguistic variations. \\

\section{Conclusions}

In this project, we successfully developed a suite of NLP models to classify ten distinct types of idiomatic expressions and literary devices. Our work demonstrated the significant evolution of transformer architectures, as we moved from a standard BERT baseline (0.63 Macro F1) to a ModernBERT implementation that achieved a near-perfect 0.97 Macro F1. 

Reflecting on our implementation, the primary challenge remained the extreme class imbalance and the inherent subjectivity of the task. Our manual annotation phase revealed that even human experts occasionally struggle to distinguish between metaphors and literal statements in "edge case" contexts. While our best models largely overcame the statistical imbalance, they are still susceptible to regional linguistic variations, such as the British specific idioms found.

For future work, we suggest exploring data augmentation techniques specifically designed for minority figurative devices. Using Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate synthetic examples of rare classes like Irony and Paradox could provide a more balanced training signal. Additionally, investigating cross- ultural robustness, specifically testing models on North American versus British idiomatic datasets, would be a valuable next step in creating more globally applicable figurative language classifiers.
